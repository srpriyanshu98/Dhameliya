Cystoscopy and Ureteroscopy

What is a Cystoscopy?

A cystoscopy is an examination inside the bladder and urethra (the tube that carries urine from the bladder to the outside of the body). In men, the urethra is the tube that runs through the penis. The doctor uses a cystoscope, a long, thin instrument with an eyepiece on one end and a tiny lens and light on the other end, to examine the bladder.

The doctor inserts the cystoscope into the patient’s urethra, allowing them to see the inner lining of the urethra and bladder. Many cystoscopes have extra channels for inserting small instruments to treat or diagnose urinary problems.

Reasons for a Cystoscopy:

  • Frequent urinary tract infections
  • Blood in the urine (hematuria)
  • A frequent and urgent need to urinate
  • Unusual cells found in a urine sample
  • Painful urination, chronic pelvic pain, or interstitial cystitis
  • Urinary blockage caused by prostate enlargement or narrowing of the urinary tract
  • Stones or unusual growths, tumors, or cancer in the lower urinary tract

What is a Ureteroscopy?

A ureteroscopy is an examination or procedure using a ureteroscope to view the inside of the urinary tract, specifically the ureters (tubes that carry urine from the kidneys to the bladder). The ureteroscope is longer and thinner than a cystoscope and can be flexible or rigid.

Doctors use a ureteroscope to see stones in the ureters and remove them or break them into smaller pieces with a laser. The procedure depends on the stone's location, size, and composition.

Reasons for a Ureteroscopy:

  • Frequent urinary tract infections
  • Hematuria
  • Unusual cells in a urine sample
  • Urinary blockage caused by narrowing of the ureter
  • Kidney stones in the ureter
  • Unusual growths, tumors, or cancer in the ureter

Preparations for the Procedure

  • Ask the doctor for specific instructions.
  • For cystoscopy, eating is usually allowed before the test. For ureteroscopy, fasting might be required.
  • Sign a consent form and ask any questions about the procedure.
  • Provide a urine sample, avoiding urination for an hour before if required.
  • Arrange for transportation if a spinal or general anesthetic is used.

Procedure Details

After applying anesthesia, the doctor inserts the cystoscope or ureteroscope through the urethra into the bladder. A sterile liquid is used to fill and stretch the bladder for better visibility. Patients might feel discomfort and an urge to urinate during this process. The procedure takes about 15 to 30 minutes, depending on the findings.

After the Procedure

  • Patients may experience mild burning during urination or notice small amounts of blood in the urine.
  • Drink plenty of water to relieve discomfort.
  • A warm bath or a warm compress may help with burning sensations.
  • Inform the doctor of any severe pain, bleeding, or signs of infection like fever or chills.

Key Points to Remember

  • Cystoscopy and ureteroscopy help view the bladder, urethra, and ureters.
  • Both procedures use specialized instruments: cystoscope and ureteroscope.
  • Follow preparation instructions and consult the doctor with any concerns.
  • Report any complications to the doctor immediately.
